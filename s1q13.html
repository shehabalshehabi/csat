Warmup Questions

  2. What is the probability that a randomly selected 22 digit number has the number 22 in it?

  4. How many binary numbers of length 8 that contains 3 11s?

CSAT Question

On a grid of m×nm×n squares, how many ways exist to get from the top-left corner to the bottom-right corner if you can only move right or down on an edge?

Hints

  1. How many steps in total must be taken?
  2. From a total of m+nm+n steps, how many are rightward steps and how many are downward steps?
  3. If we have mm rightward steps, how many combinations of them are there?
  4. For each combination of rightward steps, how many combinations of downward steps are there?

Solution

Regardless of the path taken, you must travel mm steps right and nn steps down. Of these consecutive m+nm+n steps, we consider the number of different ways we may choose mm to be rightward steps. This is (m+nm)(m+nm). For each of these combinations, the remaining nn steps down are uniquely determined i.e. there is only 1 combination since (m+n−mn)=(nn)=1(m+n−mn)=(nn)=1, so (m+nm)(m+nm) is our final answer.

Similarly, we may first find the combinations of nn downward steps and fix rightward steps to obtain (m+nn)(m+nn) as our answer.

Another way to look at this is to consider the permutations of m+nm+n steps, where mm rightward steps and nn downward steps are both indistinguishable types of steps. We find the number of permutations for m+nm+n things, and remove (by dividing by) the number of permutations specifically for just the rightwards and downward steps. This gives us the answer (m+n)!m!⋅n!(m+n)!m!⋅n!.

Thus, any of the following expressions are equivalent and correct:

(m+nm)≡(m+nn)≡(m+n)!m!⋅n!(m+nm)≡(m+nn)≡(m+n)!m!⋅n!
